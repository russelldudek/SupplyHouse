A candidate vision for turning AI from isolated tools into a people-first operating capability across SupplyHouse.

The real work

This is a change-and-value role wearing an AI title.

The tools matter. The harder work is aligning five operating conditions at once: a valuable workflow, ready data and controls, explicit human authority, an adoption owner, and evidence that the change actually helps.

SupplyHouse needs an adoption system that can move quickly without leaving people behind—and scale without turning a successful pilot into uncontrolled sprawl.

Growth

SupplyHouse entered a strategic partnership with KKR to support long-term growth while maintaining its culture.

Scale

Public company information cites more than 250,000 SKUs, 500 brands and a network reaching 98% of the U.S. population within two days.

Starting point

AI-enabled forecasting and replenishment already reduced manual work and improved supply-chain performance. The enterprise opportunity is to make that adoption repeatable across functions.

Sources: SupplyHouse role posting and company pages; SupplyHouse/KKR announcement; RELEX SupplyHouse case study. Public facts are used as context, not claims about undisclosed internal conditions.

Operating tensions

The role succeeds by managing contradictions—not choosing one side.

SpeedTrust

Move fast enough to matter.

But keep the decision authority, data boundaries and employee confidence clear enough that people will actually use what ships.

EnterpriseLocal

Reuse what should repeat.

Shared standards reduce duplication; business leaders still own the work, exceptions and consequences in their functions.

AutomationAgency

Remove toil, not dignity.

The target is stronger human capability and better outcomes—not activity metrics, tool counts or anonymous automation.

The AI Adoption Manifold

One enterprise standard. Multiple business circuits. Local control.

A manifold distributes shared capacity without forcing every circuit to behave identically. The same logic can govern enterprise AI: consistent commissioning criteria, named business owners and evidence returned to the enterprise.

All scenarios are illustrative discussion models—not assessments of SupplyHouse's current workflows.

How I would operate

Build adoption as an operating discipline.

01

Start inside the work.

Use listening sessions, Gemba-style observation and workflow evidence to understand real friction before prescribing tools.

02

Commission use cases.

Require a business owner, human-authority design, data/control review, enablement plan and measurable outcome before a pilot begins.

03

Teach through practice.

Build role-based learning around the jobs people already perform, with ambassadors, office hours, safe sandboxes and visible escalation paths.

04

Scale patterns, not demos.

Convert successful pilots into reusable playbooks, approved components, evaluation standards and clear support ownership.

05

Report outcomes and confidence.

Pair productivity and business measures with quality, risk, employee confidence, depth of use and benefit distribution across teams.

First 120 days

Earn context. Prove the mechanism. Install the capability.

The sequence is intentionally bounded. It favors trust and evidence before scale, without letting “alignment” become a euphemism for delay.

Days 0-30

Read the work

Map priorities, anxieties, existing tools, governance and workflow friction.

  • Executive and frontline listening
  • AI inventory and use-case map
  • Trust and readiness baseline
Days 31-60

Choose the circuits

Select two bounded use cases with credible value, owners and evidence.

  • Commissioning reviews
  • Business cases and risk paths
  • Ambassador cohort
Days 61-90

Run and learn

Pilot inside the workflow, support users closely and make exceptions visible.

  • Role-based enablement
  • Quality and adoption telemetry
  • Weekly decision review
Days 91-120

Install the pattern

Scale only what has earned it and formalize the enterprise operating rhythm.

  • Reusable playbook
  • CoE and decision rights
  • C-suite value narrative

Executive discovery

Questions that reveal whether adoption can compound.

Where has AI already earned trust at SupplyHouse—and what made that adoption different from the experiments that stalled?

Which business outcome would make this role undeniably successful twelve months from now?

Where should enterprise standards be non-negotiable, and where must business units retain local discretion?

How do IT, Data, Legal, HR and business leaders currently divide authority for selecting, approving, supporting and measuring AI use?

What employee concern about AI deserves to be treated as signal rather than resistance?
